Alignment

When looking at horizontal alignment, make sure that you focus on how a user wants to view the type. We prefer left aligned text. Right aligned and set justified text is not recommended except for in rare circumstances.

Left-Aligned Text

Left aligned text is used in almost all circumstances.
It is also referred to as "ragged right" alignment.

Justified Text

Justified text spaces out words and forces straight column margins on the right and left edges. The varied spacing works for newspapers, but the varied spacing makes it hard to read.

Right-Aligned Text

Right-aligned text or "ragged left" text has varied line beginnings and is generally considered hard to read. We avoid right aligning.

Centered Text

Centered text is rarely used, except for in tabs, and sometimes when labeling information. It balances underneath anchoring elements and gives the appearance of equal spacing.

Buttons

Buttons are first and foremost a functional tool. We use raised buttons (with an extremely slight shadow behind them) to give the illusion that buttons are clickable. This performs better on usability tests than completely flat buttons do. Accessibility: To ensure usability for people with disabilities, give buttons a minimum height of 36 dp and give touch targets a minimum height of 48 dp.

Sliders

Sliders are an alternative to dropdown menus, and function as multi-option conditional buttons. They should be segmented by small dark lines in between each menu option. The selected menu option should stand out- ideally in a darker color than the rest of the slider.
